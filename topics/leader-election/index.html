Leader Election

Somebody has to break ties, own the write path, and hand out work. Leader election is the machinery that picks that node, replaces it when it dies, and stops the old one from doing damage after it should have stepped aside.

Key Terms

Leader Election

The process by which a set of distributed nodes agree on exactly one node to act as the coordinator (leader) at any given time. The leader handles writes, assigns work, holds the master lock, or acts as the single decision-maker. All other nodes are followers — they forward requests to the leader or wait for instructions. When the leader fails, the remaining nodes must detect the failure and elect a new leader, ideally within seconds. Leader election appears in database replication (PostgreSQL primary election), distributed processing (Kafka partition leader), orchestration (Kubernetes controller manager), and any system where exactly-once semantics require a single coordinator. The correctness requirement: at most one leader at any time — two leaders (split-brain) can cause data corruption, duplicate processing, or conflicting decisions.

Consensus Algorithm

A protocol that allows a group of distributed nodes to agree on a single value — even if some nodes crash or messages are delayed. Paxos (Lamport, 1989/1998) is the foundational consensus algorithm but is notoriously difficult to implement correctly. Raft (Ongaro & Ousterhout, 2014) decomposes the same problem into three independent sub-problems (leader election, log replication, safety) making it practical to implement. [1] Both require a majority quorum (⌊N/2⌋ + 1 out of N nodes) to make progress — this guarantees at most one leader can be elected even during network partitions.

Lease

A time-bounded lock. A node acquires a lease (e.g., "I am the leader for the next 15 seconds"), performs leader duties, and must renew the lease before it expires. If the node crashes or becomes unreachable, the lease expires and another node can acquire it. Leases are the most common practical mechanism for leader election — etcd, ZooKeeper, and Chubby all implement leader election via leases. The critical safety property: a lease holder must stop acting as leader before the lease expires (not after), accounting for clock skew between nodes.

Fencing Token

A monotonically increasing number attached to each lease grant. When a new leader is elected, it receives a fencing token higher than any previously issued token. Storage systems reject operations bearing an old (lower) token, preventing a stale leader (one that hasn't realized its lease expired) from corrupting data. Without fencing tokens, a leader that is paused (long GC, network delay) can resume and issue writes after a new leader has already taken over — causing split-brain at the storage layer. [3]

Core Intuition

A leader simplifies distributed coordination by turning a multi-writer problem into a single-writer problem. Instead of N nodes trying to agree on every decision (expensive consensus per operation), one node makes all decisions and the others follow. The cost: the leader is a single point of failure — so you need a protocol to detect leader failure and elect a replacement quickly. Leader election is not about choosing the best node — it's about ensuring exactly one node acts as leader at any time.

02

The FLP Impossibility Result

Fischer, Lynch, and Paterson (1985) proved that in an asynchronous distributed system where even one node can crash, no deterministic algorithm can guarantee consensus will be reached in bounded time. This is the FLP impossibility result — the theoretical foundation for understanding why leader election is hard.

What FLP Means in Practice

FLP doesn't mean consensus is impossible — it means it can't be guaranteed in all cases. Practical algorithms like Raft work around FLP by using randomized timeouts (breaking the deterministic requirement) and assuming partial synchrony (the network is eventually timely, even if it's not always). In practice, Raft leader elections converge in 10-500ms under normal conditions. Under extreme network instability, elections may take longer — but FLP says they will eventually succeed, just not within a guaranteed bound. This is why leader election systems have configurable election timeouts and heartbeat intervals.

Leader Election via Raft

Raft is the most widely deployed leader election algorithm in modern systems. [1]

Raft leader election — step by step
STEADY STATE
Leader sends heartbeats (AppendEntries RPCs) to all followers every 100ms.
Followers reset their election timer on each heartbeat.

LEADER FAILURE DETECTED
Follower's election timer expires (randomized: 150-300ms).
The randomization prevents all followers from starting elections simultaneously.

ELECTION
1. Follower increments its term (logical clock) and transitions to candidate state.
2. Candidate votes for itself and sends RequestVote RPCs to all other nodes.
3. Each node votes for at most one candidate per term (first-come-first-served).
4. Candidate wins if it receives votes from a majority (⌊N/2⌋ + 1).

SAFETY: WHY SPLIT-BRAIN IS IMPOSSIBLE
- A majority of 5 nodes = 3. Two candidates can't both get 3 votes — there are only 5.
- Any network partition into [2] and [3]: only the [3] side can elect a leader.
- The Election Restriction: a candidate's log must be at least as up-to-date
  as the voter's log — ensuring committed entries are never lost.

RESULT
New leader starts sending heartbeats. Old leader (if still alive) sees a higher
term in the heartbeat and steps down to follower.
Cluster SizeMajority NeededCan Tolerate FailuresTypical Election Time
3 nodes21 node failure~150-300ms
5 nodes32 node failures~150-300ms

Leader Election via etcd Leases

etcd provides a higher-level API for leader election built on its consensus-backed key-value store. The pattern uses leases — time-bounded keys that auto-delete when the lease expires.

etcd lease-based leader election — the mechanism
ACQUIRE LEADERSHIP
1. Node creates a lease with TTL (e.g., 15 seconds).
2. Node tries to create a key (e.g., /election/leader) with its name as the value,
   attached to the lease, using a transaction:
   IF key /election/leader does not exist
   THEN create it with value = "node-3", lease = lease_id
   ELSE fail (another node is already leader)
3. If the transaction succeeds → this node is the leader.
4. If it fails → this node is a follower. It watches the key for deletion.

MAINTAIN LEADERSHIP
Leader periodically calls LeaseKeepAlive to refresh the lease TTL.
If the leader crashes → it stops refreshing → the lease expires
→ the key is auto-deleted → watchers are notified → new election starts.

FAILOVER
All followers are watching /election/leader for deletion events.
When the key is deleted (lease expired), all followers race to create the key.
etcd's linearizable transactions guarantee exactly one succeeds.
etcd vs Raw Raft for Leader Election

Raw Raft: Built into the application. Each instance participates in Raft directly. Good when you're building a replicated state machine (like a database). Complex to implement correctly. etcd: External coordination service. Your application talks to etcd via gRPC, uses its election API. Good for application-level leader election (cron jobs, partition assignment, singleton services). Much simpler — etcd handles all the consensus complexity. The trade-off: dependency on an external service (if etcd goes down, no elections can happen).

Leader Election via ZooKeeper Ephemeral Nodes

ZooKeeper implements leader election using ephemeral sequential nodes — nodes that auto-delete when the client session ends, with an auto-incrementing sequence number.

ZooKeeper recipe for leader election
THE ALGORITHM (ZooKeeper's "recipes" documentation)

1. Each candidate creates an ephemeral sequential node:
   /election/candidate-0000000001  (node A)
   /election/candidate-0000000002  (node B)
   /election/candidate-0000000003  (node C)

2. Each node lists all children of /election/ and checks:
   Am I the node with the lowest sequence number?
   - Yes → I am the leader.
   - No  → I watch the node with the next-lower sequence number.

3. Node A (0001) is the leader. B watches A. C watches B.

FAILOVER
If node A crashes → its ephemeral node is auto-deleted.
Node B's watch fires → B checks: am I now the lowest? → Yes → B is the new leader.
Node C is unaffected (still watching B).

WHY "WATCH THE NEXT-LOWER" INSTEAD OF "WATCH THE LEADER"?
If all N nodes watch the leader, the leader's deletion triggers N watches
simultaneously — a "herd effect" where all nodes race to become leader.
Watching only the next-lower node creates a chain: only one node is notified
per failure, and leadership cascades naturally. O(1) notifications, not O(N).

Fencing Tokens — Preventing Stale Leaders

The most dangerous failure in leader election isn't a dead leader — it's a leader that doesn't know it's been replaced. A node can acquire a lease, get paused (long GC, network partition), and resume after its lease has expired and a new leader has been elected. The old leader still thinks it's in charge and issues writes — corrupting the new leader's state. [3]

Fencing token protocol
WITHOUT FENCING TOKENS (dangerous)
t=0:  Node A acquires lease, becomes leader (lease expires at t=15)
t=5:  Node A starts a long GC pause (frozen for 20 seconds)
t=15: Lease expires. Node B becomes leader.
t=16: Node B writes "x = 42" to the database.
t=25: Node A wakes up from GC. Still thinks it's leader (doesn't know lease expired).
      Node A writes "x = 17" to the database. ← CORRUPTS Node B's write!

WITH FENCING TOKENS (safe)
t=0:  Node A acquires lease with token=33. Writes include token=33.
t=15: Lease expires. Node B acquires lease with token=34.
t=16: Node B writes "x = 42" with token=34. Database records max_token=34.
t=25: Node A wakes up, sends write "x = 17" with token=33.
      Database sees 33 < 34 → REJECTS the write. Data is safe.

The fencing token must be monotonically increasing and the storage layer
must enforce "reject writes with tokens lower than the highest seen."
This Is the #1 Leader Election Gotcha in Interviews

If an interviewer asks "how do you handle a leader that doesn't know it's been replaced?" — the answer is fencing tokens. Leases alone are insufficient because they rely on clock agreement between the leader and the lease grantor. A leader with a slow clock, a long GC pause, or a network partition can act on an expired lease. Fencing tokens move the safety guarantee from the leader to the storage layer — the database (or any downstream system) rejects stale operations, regardless of what the leader believes. This is Kleppmann's key argument in DDIA Ch. 8. [3]

Mini-Project: Lease-Based Leader Election

Simple: Feel What Leader Election Does

lease_election.py — in-memory lease-based election with fencing tokens (macOS/Linux/WSL)
import time, threading, random

class LeaseStore:
    """Simulates an etcd-like lease store with fencing tokens."""
    def __init__(self):
        self.leader = None
        self.lease_expiry = 0
        self.fencing_token = 0
        self.lock = threading.Lock()

    def try_acquire(self, node_id, ttl=5):
        with self.lock:
            now = time.time()
            if self.leader is None or now > self.lease_expiry:
                self.leader = node_id
                self.lease_expiry = now + ttl
                self.fencing_token += 1
                return self.fencing_token  # Returns token on success
            return None  # Already taken

    def renew(self, node_id, ttl=5):
        with self.lock:
            if self.leader == node_id:
                self.lease_expiry = time.time() + ttl
                return True
            return False

store = LeaseStore()

def node(node_id):
    while True:
        token = store.try_acquire(node_id)
        if token:
            print(f"[{node_id}] ★ Elected leader (token={token})")
            while store.renew(node_id):
                time.sleep(1)
                # Simulate random crash (10% chance)
                if random.random() < 0.1:
                    print(f"[{node_id}] ✗ Crashed! Lease will expire.")
                    time.sleep(6)  # Exceeds TTL → lease expires
                    break
        else:
            time.sleep(0.5)  # Retry

# Start 3 nodes competing for leadership
for name in ["node-A", "node-B", "node-C"]:
    threading.Thread(target=node, args=(name,), daemon=True).start()

time.sleep(20)  # Let it run for 20 seconds
Expected output (non-deterministic — leadership transfers on crash)
[node-A] ★ Elected leader (token=1)
[node-A] ✗ Crashed! Lease will expire.
[node-B] ★ Elected leader (token=2)
[node-B] ✗ Crashed! Lease will expire.
[node-C] ★ Elected leader (token=3)
... leadership transfers automatically, fencing tokens always increase ...

Production: Google Chubby

Chubby is Google's distributed lock service, published at OSDI 2006. [2] It's the system that GFS, BigTable, and MapReduce all depend on for leader election and coordination.

How Chubby Implements Leader Election

Paxos-based replication: A Chubby cell consists of 5 replicas. One is elected master via Paxos. The master handles all reads and writes; replicas replicate the log. The master holds a master lease — it can serve reads without Paxos round-trips as long as the lease is valid (typically a few seconds). This optimization reduces read latency dramatically.

Client sessions + KeepAlives: Clients maintain sessions with Chubby via periodic KeepAlive RPCs. The KeepAlive extends the session lease and also carries cache invalidation notifications piggy-backed by the master. If the master fails, a new master is elected via Paxos, and clients reconnect — the failover is designed to be invisible to most clients (existing locks and open handles are preserved across master changes).

Advisory locks + sequencers: Chubby locks are advisory (processes choose to respect them). To prevent stale locks, Chubby issues sequencers — opaque byte strings containing the lock name, mode, and generation number. Servers pass sequencers with their requests; the receiving service validates the sequencer with Chubby to confirm the lock is still held. This is Google's implementation of fencing tokens.

Production: Kafka KRaft

Apache Kafka historically depended on ZooKeeper for broker metadata and partition leader election. KRaft (Kafka Raft, introduced in Kafka 3.3, production-ready in 3.6) replaces ZooKeeper with an embedded Raft-based consensus protocol.

How KRaft Works

Controller quorum: A set of 3 or 5 broker nodes form the KRaft controller quorum. One is elected the active controller via Raft. The active controller stores all cluster metadata (topic configurations, partition assignments, broker registrations) in a replicated metadata log.

Partition leaders: For each topic partition, the controller assigns one broker as the partition leader. When a broker fails, the controller reassigns its partitions' leadership to surviving brokers. With ZooKeeper, this involved ZK watches and multiple round-trips; with KRaft, the controller already has the metadata in its Raft log — reassignment is a local operation, completing in milliseconds.

Why remove ZooKeeper? ZooKeeper was an external dependency that limited Kafka's scalability (ZK struggles with >100K partitions), added operational complexity (two systems to manage), and introduced latency for controller operations (ZK round-trips). KRaft eliminates all three problems.

Edge Cases & Gotchas

1. GC Pauses Causing False Failover

A JVM-based leader (Kafka, ZooKeeper) enters a long GC pause (e.g., 10 seconds for a full GC). The coordination service (etcd/ZK) decides the leader is dead and elects a new one. When GC finishes, the old leader resumes — now there are two leaders. Fix: tune GC to avoid long pauses (use ZGC or Shenandoah), increase lease TTL to exceed max GC pause, and always use fencing tokens.

2. Network Partition — Asymmetric

Node A can reach etcd but not Node B. Node B can reach etcd but not Node A. Both believe they're healthy. If A is the leader, B may think A is dead (can't reach it directly) and try to acquire leadership. But etcd still has A's lease — B fails. The system works correctly because leadership is determined by etcd (not by peer-to-peer connectivity). This is why using an external coordination service is safer than peer-to-peer leader election.

3. Clock Skew in Lease-Based Systems

Node A's clock runs 5 seconds fast. It acquires a 10-second lease. Node A thinks the lease expires at wall-clock 12:00:10. But etcd (with correct time) thinks it expires at 12:00:05. At 12:00:06, etcd grants the lease to Node B. At 12:00:07, both A and B think they're the leader. Fix: (1) Use NTP to keep clocks synchronized within milliseconds. (2) Leaders should stop work slightly before lease expiry (safety margin). (3) Always use fencing tokens — clocks are not reliable enough for safety-critical coordination.

4. Thundering Herd on Leader Failure

When the leader dies, all followers simultaneously try to become leader. With N nodes hitting etcd simultaneously, the contention can slow down the election. ZooKeeper's ephemeral sequential node pattern (watch only the next-lower node) elegantly avoids this. etcd's campaign API serializes election attempts. Raw Raft uses randomized timeouts to desynchronize candidates.

5. Leader Election Without a Coordination Service

Some teams attempt leader election using database row locks, Redis SETNX, or file system locks. These are fragile: database locks don't survive connection drops (lock released when connection closes, but lock holder may not know), Redis SETNX has no built-in fencing tokens, and file locks don't work across machines. Use etcd or ZooKeeper — they exist specifically to solve this problem correctly.

What If? — Interactive Exploration

Tradeoff Cards

Embedded Raft vs external etcd

Embedded Raft: No external dependency. The application IS the consensus group. Best for databases (CockroachDB, TiKV) where the data plane and control plane are unified.
External etcd/ZK: Separate coordination service. Simpler for application-level leader election (cron jobs, singleton services). But another system to operate.
Decision: Building a replicated data store → embed Raft. Need a leader among stateless workers → use etcd.

Short lease TTL vs long lease TTL

Short (2-5s): Fast failover (new leader in seconds). But leader must refresh frequently (more RPCs), and transient network blips cause unnecessary elections.
Long (30-60s): Tolerant of network jitter. But failover takes 30-60 seconds — the system is leaderless (and stalled) for that duration.
Typical production: 10-15 second leases with 3-5 second refresh intervals.

3-node vs 5-node consensus cluster

3 nodes: Majority = 2. Tolerates 1 failure. Cheaper, simpler. But a 2-node failure → no quorum → system stalls.
5 nodes: Majority = 3. Tolerates 2 failures. More resilient. But 5 nodes = more hardware, slightly higher commit latency (need 3 ACKs instead of 2).
Rule: 3 for development/staging. 5 for production Kubernetes etcd clusters.

Leader-based vs leaderless systems

Leader-based: Single writer, simpler consistency. But leader is a bottleneck and single point of failure (mitigated by fast election).
Leaderless (Dynamo-style): Any node accepts writes. No election needed. But conflict resolution is complex (vector clocks, CRDTs).
Decision: Need strong consistency → leader. Need maximum availability → leaderless.

Interview Questions & Answers

Q1: What is leader election and why is it needed?

Leader election is the process by which distributed nodes agree on exactly one node to act as coordinator. It's needed because many distributed operations require a single decision-maker: database writes (single-leader replication), partition assignment (Kafka controller), distributed locks (mutual exclusion), and singleton tasks (cron jobs that should run on exactly one instance). Without leader election, either every node must coordinate on every decision (expensive consensus per operation) or no coordination happens (data corruption, duplicate work). The key safety property: at most one leader at any time. Two leaders (split-brain) is worse than zero leaders (temporary unavailability).

Q2: How does Raft prevent two leaders from existing simultaneously?

Raft uses three mechanisms: (1) Majority vote: a candidate must receive votes from ⌊N/2⌋+1 nodes. In a 5-node cluster, that's 3 votes. Two candidates can't both get 3 out of 5 votes. (2) One vote per term: each node votes for at most one candidate in a given term (first-come-first-served). If a node already voted for candidate A in term 5, it rejects candidate B's request for term 5. (3) Term number: if a leader discovers a higher term in any message, it immediately steps down to follower. This ensures that even if a network partition temporarily creates two leaders, the one with the lower term will step down when the partition heals. The combination guarantees: exactly one leader per term, at most one term active at a time.

Q3: What are fencing tokens and why are leases alone insufficient?

Leases grant leadership for a bounded time, but a leader can be paused (GC, network delay) and resume after its lease expired, unaware that a new leader was elected. If the old leader writes to the database, it corrupts the new leader's state. Fencing tokens solve this: each lease grant carries a monotonically increasing number. The database rejects any write with a token lower than the highest it has seen. When the old leader resumes with token=33, but the new leader has been writing with token=34, the database rejects the old leader's writes. This moves the safety guarantee from the leader (which may have an incorrect view of time) to the storage layer (which enforces a strict ordering). DDIA Ch. 8 argues this is the only safe approach for distributed locks.

Q4: Compare etcd, ZooKeeper, and Redis for leader election.

etcd: Raft-based, strongly consistent, native lease + election API, gRPC interface, K8s native. The modern standard for leader election. ZooKeeper: ZAB-based, strongly consistent, ephemeral nodes for sessions, mature (15+ years), but heavier operationally and being phased out (Kafka KRaft, Solr removing ZK). Redis: Tempting (SETNX + EXPIRE = "leader election in one line"), but fundamentally unsafe: Redis replication is async — a failover can lose the lock key, allowing two leaders. The Redlock algorithm attempts to fix this with multi-instance coordination, but Kleppmann demonstrated it's still unsafe under realistic failure scenarios (long GC pauses, clock drift). Use etcd or ZooKeeper for leader election. Do not use Redis unless you accept the risk of split-brain.

Q5: A distributed cron scheduler runs on 5 instances. How do you ensure each scheduled job runs on exactly one instance?

Approach 1 — Global leader: Elect one instance as the leader using etcd. Only the leader runs cron jobs. If the leader dies, a new leader is elected and takes over the schedule. Simple but creates a bottleneck (all jobs run on one machine). Approach 2 — Per-job leader: Each job has its own leader lock in etcd (key: /cron/jobs/{job_id}/leader). Instances compete for each job independently. Different jobs can run on different instances — natural load distribution. Approach 3 — Database-backed locks: Each instance tries INSERT INTO cron_locks (job_id, instance_id, expires_at) WHERE NOT EXISTS ... with a TTL. The database guarantees exactly one insert succeeds. Simpler but requires the database to be available. For FAANG-scale: Approach 2 with etcd. It's the pattern used by Kubernetes CronJobs (the controller is the leader that decides which node runs each job).

Interviewer Follow-ups

"A worker takes a task, starts processing, then crashes. How do you handle this?"

The scheduler tracks task state: pending → assigned → completed. When a task is assigned to a worker, the scheduler records the assignment with a deadline (e.g., "must complete within 60 seconds"). If the worker doesn't report completion by the deadline, the scheduler reassigns the task to another worker with a new, higher fencing token. If the original worker recovers and tries to mark the task complete with the old token, the Task DB rejects the write (stale token). This is the at-least-once + idempotency = exactly-once pattern. The task itself should be idempotent (sending the same email twice is the same as sending it once, using a deduplication key). Reference: DDIA Ch. 11 discusses this as the "effectively exactly-once" pattern — true exactly-once requires both the scheduler and the downstream system to participate in the deduplication protocol.

"The scheduler leader crashes. What happens to the in-flight tasks?"

The scheduler's etcd lease expires. A standby scheduler instance acquires the leader lease and becomes the new scheduler. The new scheduler reads the task state from the Task DB: tasks marked "assigned" with expired deadlines are reassigned. Tasks marked "pending" are assigned normally. Tasks marked "completed" are skipped. The key: all task state lives in the Task DB, not in the scheduler's memory. The scheduler is stateless — it's purely a coordinator. Any scheduler instance can take over by reading the DB. This is why the scheduler holds an etcd leader lease but stores task state in a durable database (PostgreSQL), not in etcd (which has a 2GB limit and isn't designed for high-volume state).

"How do you handle scheduled/delayed tasks — 'send this email at 3pm tomorrow'?"

Store tasks with an execute_at timestamp in the Task DB. The scheduler periodically polls: SELECT * FROM tasks WHERE execute_at <= NOW() AND status = 'pending' ORDER BY execute_at LIMIT 100. Alternatively, use a delay queue pattern: tasks are inserted into a Redis Sorted Set with score = execute_at timestamp. The scheduler does ZRANGEBYSCORE tasks -inf {now} LIMIT 100 to find tasks ready for execution. The Redis approach is faster for high-volume scheduling (sorted set lookup is O(log N)), while the PostgreSQL approach is simpler and doesn't require another system. For 10K tasks/second, Redis or a dedicated queue (Amazon SQS with delay) is more appropriate than polling PostgreSQL.

What the Interviewer Is Testing

(1) Leader election usage — exactly one scheduler active at a time, using etcd leases. (2) Fencing tokens for safety — preventing stale workers and stale schedulers from corrupting task state. (3) Failure recovery — stateless scheduler + durable task DB means any scheduler can take over. (4) Exactly-once semantics — at-least-once delivery + idempotent tasks + fencing tokens. (5) Separation of coordination (etcd) from state (PostgreSQL) — using each system for what it's designed for.

14

Key Takeaways Cheat Sheet

The 10 Things You Must Know

1. Leader election ensures exactly one node acts as coordinator. Two leaders (split-brain) is worse than no leader (temporary unavailability).
2. Raft: majority vote + one vote per term + term numbers → at most one leader per term. Used by etcd, CockroachDB, TiKV, Kafka KRaft.
3. etcd leases: create a key with a TTL. If the holder crashes, the key auto-deletes. Watchers are notified. Simple, safe, production-ready.
4. ZooKeeper ephemeral sequential nodes: watch the next-lower node to avoid herd effect. Chain of watchers → O(1) notifications per failure.
5. Fencing tokens prevent stale leaders from corrupting data. The storage layer rejects operations with tokens lower than the highest seen.
6. Leases alone are insufficient — a paused leader (GC, network) can act on an expired lease. Always combine leases with fencing tokens.
7. FLP impossibility: deterministic consensus is impossible in async systems with even one crash. Raft works around it with randomized timeouts.
8. Google Chubby: Paxos-based lock service. GFS, BigTable, MapReduce all depend on it. Sequencers (fencing tokens) prevent stale locks.
9. Kafka KRaft replaces ZooKeeper with embedded Raft. Controller quorum holds all metadata. Faster partition reassignment, simpler operations.
10. Do NOT use Redis for leader election (async replication → split-brain risk). Use etcd or ZooKeeper — they exist to solve this problem.

The safety chain is layered: elect one leader, expire that leader when it stops renewing, then block stale writes even if the old leader wakes back up.
Key Intuition

Leader election requires three layers of safety, not one. Consensus (Raft) ensures at most one leader is elected. Leases ensure a dead leader eventually loses power. Fencing tokens ensure a stale leader can't corrupt data even if it doesn't know it's been replaced. Each layer addresses a different failure mode. Missing any one layer creates a window for split-brain or data corruption. In interviews, demonstrating awareness of all three layers — especially fencing tokens — signals senior-level distributed systems understanding.

References & Further Reading

Use these as mechanism notes, not reading list items: each source is here because it explains a specific failure mode, invariant, or protocol move that shows up in real systems and interviews.

[1] Ongaro & Ousterhout — "In Search of an Understandable Consensus Algorithm" (USENIX ATC 2014)

Diego Ongaro, John Ousterhout. Stanford University. USENIX ATC 2014, pp. 305-319. Best Paper.
https://raft.github.io/raft.pdf

• The election path is really a timer design problem: heartbeats reset follower timers, timeout randomness breaks symmetry, and a candidate only needs one clean majority round to take over. That is the concrete reason Raft avoids the endless election collisions that show up in naive peer-to-peer schemes.

• The vote rule is also a log-safety rule. A follower does not vote purely because a timeout fired; it first checks whether the candidate's last term,index is at least as up to date as its own. That single check is what stops an older node from becoming leader and throwing away already-committed entries.

• Read this paper for the exact split-brain boundary: majority quorum + one vote per term + higher-term stepdown. Those three rules are the core of why a 5-node cluster can tolerate two failures but a 2/3 partition cannot keep making decisions on both sides.

• The useful implementation extras are the ones operators actually feel: pre-vote to stop stale rejoining nodes from disturbing a healthy leader, and leadership transfer to move primaries deliberately during maintenance instead of forcing a messy timeout-driven failover.

• Come back to the formal model when you want to sanity-check an implementation claim. Raft is simple enough to explain in prose, but still subtle enough that the TLA+ model and dissertation extensions are worth using as the final correctness reference.

[2] Burrows — "The Chubby Lock Service for Loosely-Coupled Distributed Systems" (OSDI 2006)

Mike Burrows. Google Inc. 7th USENIX OSDI, November 2006.
https://research.google.com/archive/chubby-osdi06.pdf

• Chubby matters because it shows how a production lock service turns Paxos into a lease-backed master. The master is not just "the node that won"; it is the node that can safely serve reads locally because its lease is still backed by quorum-confirmed state.

• The KeepAlive path is worth studying as a protocol, not a feature. Session renewal, cache invalidation, and liveness checking all ride the same RPC stream, which keeps the client model simple and gives the master one place to decide who is still alive enough to trust.

• The paper's most useful locking detail is the sequencer. Chubby does not assume every client instantly notices a revoked lock; instead it hands downstream systems a generation-bearing token they can validate. That is the practical ancestor of the fencing-token pattern used in modern leader-election answers.

• Read the operational numbers as a design constraint: coordination systems spend most of their lives hiding failover from clients, not proving liveness on a whiteboard. Fast master replacement, session continuity, and cache behavior matter more than theoretical elegance once hundreds of internal systems depend on the service.

• The "used as a name service" lesson is also technical, not cultural. Once teams started reading Chubby paths like service-discovery records, the read path and cache layer became first-class scaling problems. That is a reminder that coordination planes often get dragged into data-plane traffic if the API makes it too easy.

[3] Kleppmann — "Designing Data-Intensive Applications" (2017), Ch. 8-9

Martin Kleppmann. O'Reilly Media, 2017.
https://dataintensive.net/

• The main lesson to extract here is blunt: a lease is a liveness hint, not a safety proof. GC pauses, delayed packets, and clock skew are enough to let an old leader keep talking after a new one has taken over. Safety only comes back when the storage layer compares a monotonically increasing fencing value and rejects stale work.

• The Redlock discussion is useful because it names the exact failure assumptions that break casual lock designs: asynchronous replication, local clocks, and unbounded process pauses. If your lock service depends on those behaving nicely, it is not really a correctness primitive.

• Chapter 9 is where leader election stops being "just picking a primary" and turns into a bigger equivalence class. Leader election, total order broadcast, atomic commit, and linearizable compare-and-swap are tightly related because they all require one globally agreed ordering of decisions.

• This is also the right place to internalize the clock model. Wall-clock time is for expiries and operator dashboards; protocol correctness has to hang off logical counters such as terms, epochs, lease generations, and fencing tokens.

• Keep FLP in view as the background constraint: a leader-election system can be safe or live under harsh conditions, but not both without extra timing assumptions. That is why production systems talk about election timeouts, heartbeat intervals, and partial synchrony instead of promising instant failover under every network failure.
