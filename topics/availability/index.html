System Design Fundamentals · Topic 05

Availability

Your system is only as valuable as the minutes it's actually working. A database that's 10× faster than the competition is worthless if it's down when your customers need it. Availability is the art of keeping systems alive — measured in nines, defended with redundancy, and promised in SLAs.

Key Terms

Process

A program currently executing on a machine. In any sufficiently large distributed system, you should operate under the assumption that any process can be terminated at any moment — by hardware failure, OS kill signals, resource exhaustion, or deployment rollouts.

Node / Instance / Host

These terms are largely interchangeable — they all refer to a virtual or physical machine that runs processes. In cloud environments, an "instance" usually means a VM (like an EC2 instance), while "node" is more common in Kubernetes and cluster contexts. The word "server" often refers to the same thing.

Availability

The probability that a system is operational and responding correctly at any given moment. Expressed as a percentage — a system with 99% availability is expected to be functioning 99% of the time. The number of nines in that percentage (e.g., 99.99% = "four nines") is the standard shorthand for how reliable a system is.

High Availability (HA)

A design approach for systems that must remain operational with minimal downtime through redundancy, failover, and removal of single points of failure. The exact target varies by product: some teams call 99.9% highly available, others reserve the term for four or five nines. The important idea is architectural, not ceremonial: failures should happen without taking the whole service down.

Nines

Shorthand for the number of 9s in an availability percentage. Each additional nine reduces allowed downtime by roughly 10×:

NinesAvailabilityDowntime/YearDowntime/MonthDowntime/Day
Two 9s99%3.65 days7.31 hours14.4 min
Three 9s99.9%8.77 hours43.8 min1.44 min
Four 9s99.99%52.6 min4.38 min8.6 sec
Five 9s99.999%5.26 min26.3 sec0.86 sec

Redundancy

Duplicating critical components — servers, databases, network paths, entire data centers — so that when one fails, another takes over. Redundancy is the primary mechanism for achieving high availability. It comes at the cost of complexity and money.

SLA (Service-Level Agreement)

A formal contract between a service provider and its customers that specifies guaranteed performance levels. An SLA bundles one or more SLOs and typically includes consequences (credits, refunds) if the guarantees are breached. For example, AWS guarantees S3 at 99.9% availability; if they miss it, you get service credits.

SLO (Service-Level Objective)

A specific, measurable target within an SLA. An SLO might state: "API response time p99 will be under 200ms" or "monthly uptime will be at least 99.95%." SLOs are what engineers actually monitor and alert on — they're the operational targets that roll up into the business-level SLA.

SLI (Service-Level Indicator)

The actual measured value that an SLO is evaluated against. If the SLO says "99.9% of requests will succeed," the SLI is the real-time ratio of successful requests to total requests. SLIs are the raw metrics; SLOs are the thresholds; SLAs are the contracts.

01

What Is Availability & Why It Matters

Availability answers one question: "if I try to use this system right now, will it work?" It's the most user-visible quality attribute of any system. Users don't notice if your database uses B-trees or LSM-trees. They absolutely notice when the page shows a 500 error.

Core Intuition

Think of a vending machine. Availability is the percentage of the time you walk up to it and it actually dispenses your drink. If it's out of order 1 day out of 100, that's 99% availability (two nines). If it works every single time except for 5 minutes a year, that's five nines. The engineering challenge: vending machines break. The availability challenge: making sure there's always another machine ready when one breaks.

Not All Systems Need the Same Availability

A critical insight for system design interviews: availability requirements vary by use case. Over-engineering availability wastes money. Under-engineering it loses customers. The right answer depends on what happens when the system is down:

SystemTypical TargetImpact of Downtime
Personal blog~99% (two 9s)Nobody notices. You lose a few page views.
Internal dashboard~99.5%Engineers grumble, switch to manual queries.
E-commerce checkout99.95% (three-and-half 9s)Lost sales. Every minute down = lost revenue.
Payment processing (Stripe)99.99% (four 9s)Transactions fail. Merchants lose trust.
AWS S3 / Core infra99.99%+Thousands of downstream services break.
911 emergency system99.999% (five 9s)People could die. Regulated availability.
02

Measuring Availability — The Nines

Availability is typically measured in two ways:

Time-based: What fraction of a given period was the system operational?
Availability = Uptime / (Uptime + Downtime)

Request-based: What fraction of requests received a successful response?
Availability = Successful Requests / Total Requests

The request-based definition is more useful for modern distributed systems because a service can be "up" but returning errors to 5% of users due to a partial failure (one node down, one region degraded).

The Cost of Each Nine

Each additional nine is roughly 10× harder and 10× more expensive to achieve than the previous one. Going from 99% to 99.9% might require adding a standby replica. Going from 99.9% to 99.99% might require multi-region deployment. Going from 99.99% to 99.999% might require custom hardware, redundant network paths, and a 24/7 on-call team with sub-minute response times. The engineering effort grows exponentially — pick the right target for your use case, don't blindly chase five nines.

SLAs, SLOs, and SLIs — The Contract

These three terms form a hierarchy that connects business promises to engineering metrics. Understanding them precisely matters in interviews and in real operations.

THE SLA/SLO/SLI HIERARCHY │ ├── SLA (Service-Level Agreement) │ The business contract. "We guarantee X, or we pay you Y." │ Example: "99.95% monthly uptime, or customer gets 10% credit." │ Who cares: Legal, Sales, Customers. │ ├── SLO (Service-Level Objective) │ The internal engineering target. Typically stricter than the SLA. │ Example: "99.97% monthly uptime" (gives breathing room above SLA). │ Who cares: Engineering, SRE, On-call teams. │ └── SLI (Service-Level Indicator) The raw metric measured in real time. Example: "ratio of HTTP 2xx responses to total responses, measured per minute." Who cares: Monitoring systems, dashboards, alerting pipelines. THE ERROR BUDGET │ └── If your SLO is 99.95%, you have a monthly error budget of: 30 days × 24h × 60m × 0.05% = ~21.6 minutes of allowed downtime When the budget runs out → freeze deployments, fix reliability first. This is the core mechanism of Google's SRE practice. [1]
The Error Budget Concept

Google's SRE book popularized the error budget: the amount of downtime your SLO permits. If your SLO is 99.95%, your monthly error budget is ~21.6 minutes. If you've used 15 minutes already this month, you have 6.6 minutes left. When the budget is exhausted, the team shifts from feature work to reliability work — no more risky deployments until the budget refills next month. This creates a quantitative framework for balancing velocity (ship features) vs reliability (don't break things). [1]

Redundancy — The Core Strategy

The primary mechanism for achieving high availability is redundancy — having multiple instances of every critical component so that when one fails, another is ready. Redundancy exists at every layer of the stack.

Types of Redundancy

LayerSingle Point of FailureRedundancy Solution
ServerOne app server crashes → service downMultiple servers behind a load balancer
DatabasePrimary DB crashes → writes failPrimary-replica replication with automatic failover
NetworkOne network switch dies → partitionRedundant switches, dual-homed NICs, BGP multi-path
PowerPower supply fails → server diesDual power supplies, UPS, backup generators
Data centerFire/flood → entire DC offlineMulti-AZ (availability zone) or multi-region deployment
DNSDNS provider down → domain unreachableSecondary DNS provider, anycast DNS
Load balancerLB crash → all traffic lostActive-passive LB pair with VRRP/keepalived

Active-Passive vs Active-Active

Active-Passive (standby): One component handles all traffic. The other sits idle, monitoring the primary. When the primary fails, the passive takes over (failover). Simpler but wastes resources — the standby is doing nothing until needed. Used for databases where only one node can accept writes.

Active-Active: All components handle traffic simultaneously. When one fails, the others absorb its share. More efficient (no idle capacity) but more complex (state synchronization, split-brain risks). Used for stateless web servers behind a load balancer.

The Hidden Cost of Redundancy

Redundancy doesn't just double your hardware cost — it adds operational complexity. Replicated databases need conflict resolution. Multi-region deployments need data synchronization. Failover mechanisms need testing (does it actually work when you need it?). Netflix spends enormous engineering effort not on adding redundancy, but on continuously testing that their redundancy works — that's the philosophy behind Chaos Engineering. [3]

05

Failover Patterns

Redundancy is useless without failover — the mechanism that detects failure and redirects traffic to the healthy component. The failover time directly determines how much downtime you experience.

Common Failover Patterns
1. DNS Failover
   Health check fails → DNS record updated to point to backup IP
   Failover time: 30s–5min (depends on DNS TTL)
   Pro: Simple. Con: Slow (TTL caching), clients may cache stale DNS.

2. Load Balancer Health Checks
   LB pings each backend every 5–10s. If 3 pings fail → mark unhealthy.
   Failover time: 15–30s
   Pro: Transparent to clients. Con: Detection delay = 3 × check interval.

3. Database Automatic Failover
   Primary sends heartbeats to replicas. Heartbeat stops → replica promotes.
   Failover time: tens of seconds to a couple of minutes
   Pro: Automatic. Con: Risk of split-brain if detection is wrong.

4. Floating IP / Virtual IP (VIP)
   Two servers share a VIP via VRRP (keepalived). Active holds the IP.
   Active dies → passive claims the IP in <3 seconds.
   Pro: Very fast. Con: Only works within same L2 network / subnet.

5. Client-Side Failover
   Client knows multiple endpoints. First fails → try the second.
   Failover time: timeout + retry = 1–5s
   Pro: No infrastructure needed. Con: Client must implement retry logic.

6. Multi-Region Failover (Global)
   Route 53 / Cloudflare health checks per region. Region fails → DNS
   routes to next-closest healthy region.
   Failover time: 30s–2min
   Pro: Survives entire DC/region failures. Con: Complex state sync.

Calculating System Availability

A system is a chain of components. Its overall availability depends on how those components are connected — in series (all must work) or in parallel (any one can work).

Components in Series (AND)

If your request goes through Server → Database → Cache, all three must be up. The overall availability is the product:

Series: A_total = A₁ × A₂ × A₃
Server: 99.9% | Database: 99.9% | Cache: 99.9%
Total = 0.999 × 0.999 × 0.999 = 0.997 = 99.7%

Three components at three 9s → system drops to under three 9s.
With 10 components in series at 99.9% each:
Total = 0.999^10 = 0.990 = 99.0%  ← lost a full nine!

Lesson: every dependency in the critical path REDUCES availability.
This is why microservices are harder to keep available than monoliths.

Components in Parallel (OR — redundancy)

If you have two replicas and only one needs to work, availability improves dramatically:

Parallel: A_total = 1 - (1 - A₁) × (1 - A₂)
One server at 99%: downtime = 1%
Two servers in parallel, each 99%:
A = 1 - (1 - 0.99) × (1 - 0.99) = 1 - 0.0001 = 99.99%

Two nines → four nines, just by adding one replica!

Three servers at 99% each:
A = 1 - (0.01)³ = 1 - 0.000001 = 99.9999%  ← six nines!

Lesson: redundancy is multiplicative when failures are independent.
The Napkin Math for Availability

In system design interviews, you'll often need to estimate availability on the fly. The rules of thumb: Series — nines subtract (roughly). Three 9s + three 9s in series ≈ 2.7 nines. Parallel — redundancy can improve availability dramatically, but only if failures are reasonably independent. Every dependency in the critical path is a tax on availability. Every well-isolated redundant replica is a boost.

Availability vs Consistency — CAP Preview

In distributed systems, availability doesn't exist in isolation — it's in tension with consistency. This tension is formalized by the CAP theorem. [2]

The CAP Theorem (Brewer, 2000)

In a distributed system experiencing a network partition, you can guarantee at most two of three properties: Consistency (every read gets the latest write), Availability (every request gets a response), and Partition tolerance (the system works despite network failures between nodes).

Since network partitions will happen in any real distributed system, the practical choice is: CP (sacrifice availability during partitions — return errors rather than stale data) or AP (sacrifice consistency during partitions — return potentially stale data rather than errors).

ChoiceDuring PartitionExample SystemsUse Case
CPReject requests to maintain consistencyZooKeeper, etcd, HBase, SpannerFinancial transactions, leader election, config stores
APServe requests with potentially stale dataCassandra, DynamoDB, CouchDB, DNSShopping carts, social feeds, session stores

Brewer himself later clarified that CAP is not a simple binary choice — it's a spectrum, and the trade-off only applies during a partition. When the network is healthy, you can have all three. Modern systems like Google Spanner push the boundaries by using synchronized clocks (TrueTime) to minimize the window where the trade-off applies. [2]

Mini-Project: Health-Check Monitor

Build a Python availability monitor that pings multiple endpoints, tracks uptime percentage, detects outages, and reports per-service SLI metrics — exactly what real monitoring tools like UptimeRobot or Datadog do at a basic level.

availability_monitor.py
import time, urllib.request, json, sys
from collections import defaultdict
from datetime import datetime

# ── Configure endpoints to monitor ──
TARGETS = {
    "GitHub API":   "https://api.github.com",
    "Google":       "https://www.google.com",
    "httpbin":      "https://httpbin.org/get",
    "LocalServer":  "http://localhost:8001",  # Will fail if nothing runs here
}
CHECK_INTERVAL = 5  # seconds between checks

stats = defaultdict(lambda: {"checks": 0, "successes": 0, "failures": 0, "last_status": "?"})

def check(name, url):
    try:
        req = urllib.request.Request(url, headers={"User-Agent": "monitor/1.0"})
        resp = urllib.request.urlopen(req, timeout=3)
        ok = 200 <= resp.status < 400
    except:
        ok = False
    stats[name]["checks"] += 1
    stats[name]["successes" if ok else "failures"] += 1
    prev = stats[name]["last_status"]
    stats[name]["last_status"] = "UP" if ok else "DOWN"
    # Detect state transitions
    if prev == "UP" and not ok:
        print(f"  OUTAGE DETECTED: {name} is DOWN!")
    elif prev == "DOWN" and ok:
        print(f"  RECOVERED: {name} is back UP")
    return ok

def report():
    print(f"\n{'='*60}")
    print(f"  AVAILABILITY REPORT — {datetime.now().strftime('%H:%M:%S')}")
    print(f"{'='*60}")
    for name, s in stats.items():
        avail = (s["successes"] / s["checks"] * 100) if s["checks"] else 0
        status = "UP" if s["last_status"] == "UP" else "DOWN"
        nines = "5+ nines" if avail >= 99.999 else "four 9s" if avail >= 99.99 \
                else "three 9s" if avail >= 99.9 else "two 9s" if avail >= 99 else "< two 9s"
        print(f"  {status} {name:<15} {avail:7.3f}% ({nines}) [{s['checks']} checks]")
    print()

print("[Monitor] Checking every 5s. Ctrl+C for report.\n")
try:
    cycle = 0
    while True:
        ts = datetime.now().strftime("%H:%M:%S")
        results = []
        for name, url in TARGETS.items():
            ok = check(name, url)
            results.append(f"{name}:{'✓' if ok else '✗'}")
        print(f"  [{ts}] {' | '.join(results)}")
        cycle += 1
        if cycle % 12 == 0: report()  # Report every ~60 seconds
        time.sleep(CHECK_INTERVAL)
except KeyboardInterrupt:
    report()
Experiments to Try

1. Start a local server on port 8001, let the monitor run, then kill the server — watch the outage detection trigger.
2. Restart the server — watch the recovery detection.
3. Let it run for 5 minutes and check the availability percentage — you'll see the LocalServer at <50% while the public APIs are at 100%.
4. Add more endpoints (your own APIs, cloud dashboards) and watch the SLIs diverge.

Production Example 1: AWS S3

S3 is one of the most critical pieces of internet infrastructure. AWS says S3 Standard is designed for 99.99% availability and 99.999999999% durability, while the S3 SLA offers service credits if monthly uptime drops below 99.9%. That distinction matters: product design targets and contractual SLA thresholds are related, but they are not the same thing. [5]

How S3 Achieves High Availability

Replication across AZs: Every object is automatically replicated across at least 3 availability zones within a region. Each AZ is a physically separate data center with independent power, networking, and cooling.

Automatic failover: If an AZ fails, requests are transparently routed to healthy AZs. Clients don't even know a failover happened.

Durability vs availability: S3 distinguishes between durability (11 nines over a year, meaning objects are extraordinarily unlikely to be lost) and availability (whether requests succeed when you need them). Your data can remain intact even during a temporary availability event. [5]

The 2017 S3 Outage

On February 28, 2017, an incorrectly entered parameter during S3 maintenance removed more server capacity than intended in us-east-1. Because other AWS systems and many customer applications depended on that regional S3 control path, the blast radius was large and recovery took several hours while key subsystems restarted. AWS later added stronger safeguards to the tooling and reprioritized work to improve recovery speed. Even extremely durable storage can still go down because of operational mistakes and slow recovery in the control path. [5]

Production Example 2: Netflix Chaos Engineering

Netflix pioneered chaos engineering — the practice of deliberately injecting failures into production to verify that the system's redundancy and failover mechanisms actually work. [3]

The Simian Army

Chaos Monkey: Randomly kills EC2 instances in production during business hours. If your service can't survive losing a random server, Chaos Monkey will expose it before a real failure does.

Chaos Kong: Simulates an entire AWS region going offline. Netflix's architecture must route all traffic to another region and continue operating. This runs periodically to verify multi-region failover works end-to-end.

Latency Monkey: Injects artificial delays into network calls between services to verify timeout and circuit-breaker configurations are correct.

The Netflix Philosophy

Netflix helped popularize the idea that resilient systems must be exercised under controlled failure, not just designed on whiteboards. The insight remains strong: untested redundancy isn't redundancy. If you've never actually killed a dependency and observed the failover path, you don't really know it works. Chaos engineering turns hope into evidence. [3]

Edge Cases & Gotchas

1. Split-Brain

When a network partition causes two nodes to both believe they're the primary (leader). Both accept writes, creating conflicting data. When the partition heals, you have two divergent datasets. Fix: quorum-based leader election (majority must agree), fencing tokens, or STONITH (Shoot The Other Node In The Head — a real term in HA clustering).

2. Cascading Failures

Service A fails → Service B (which depends on A) starts timing out → B's thread pool exhausts → B fails → Service C (depends on B) fails → entire system collapses. Fix: circuit breakers (stop calling a failing service after N failures), bulkheads (isolate failure domains), timeouts (don't wait forever), and graceful degradation (return partial results instead of failing entirely).

3. Thundering Herd After Recovery

System recovers from an outage. All queued requests flood in simultaneously. The freshly-recovered system is immediately overwhelmed and goes back down. Fix: rate limiting on recovery, gradual traffic ramp-up (like Envoy's slow start), and jittered client retries.

4. Partial Failures Are Harder Than Total Failures

A totally dead server is easy to detect and fail over from. A server that's up but slow (brownout) or returning errors for some requests (gray failure) is much harder. Health checks pass because the server is alive, but users are suffering. Fix: deep health checks (test actual functionality, not just /health returning 200), anomaly detection on latency/error-rate, and request-level load balancing.

5. The "Works on My Machine" Disaster Recovery Test

A backup that hasn't been tested is not a backup. A failover mechanism that's never been triggered is a hope, not a guarantee. Regular disaster recovery drills are essential. Mature reliability organizations run them routinely; many teams don't, and discover their DR plan is broken during the one time they need it.

Production War Story

On October 4, 2021, Meta suffered a multi-hour outage after configuration changes on backbone routers disrupted communication between data centers. Public-facing services and many internal tools were affected at the same time, which made diagnosis and recovery harder. The broader point is simple: do not let the recovery path, the control plane, and your internal tooling all depend on the same failing layer. [6]

Tradeoff Cards

Adding a replica

Improves: Availability and fault tolerance when failures are independent.
Costs: 2× hardware spend, data sync complexity, potential stale reads (if AP), split-brain risk.
When worth it: Your SLO is being missed, or a single failure causes total outage.

Multi-region deployment

Improves: Survives entire DC/region failures, lower latency for global users.
Costs: Cross-region data replication latency (+50-150ms), consistency challenges, 3-5× operational complexity.
When worth it: You need four+ nines, or your users are geographically distributed.

Faster failover detection

Improves: Reduces downtime per incident (10s → 3s detection = 7s less per failure).
Costs: More aggressive health checks = more false positives (flapping), increased network overhead.
When worth it: You're within budget on total incidents but each one eats too much error budget.

Circuit breakers on dependencies

Improves: Prevents cascading failure. Isolates blast radius. Enables graceful degradation.
Costs: Users get partial/degraded responses during the open-circuit window. More complex error handling.
When worth it: Always — any service calling external dependencies should have circuit breakers.

Toy Simulation: Failure Cascade Probability

How likely is at least one component to fail in a system with N components, each at availability A? This is the math behind why microservices are harder to keep available than monoliths.

failure_cascade.py — run this to see the math viscerally
import random

def simulate(n_components, avail_each, n_replicas, trials=100_000):
    """Monte Carlo simulation of system availability."""
    system_up = 0
    for _ in range(trials):
        all_components_ok = True
        for _ in range(n_components):
            # Component is up if ANY replica is up (parallel)
            component_up = any(
                random.random() < avail_each for _ in range(n_replicas)
            )
            if not component_up:
                all_components_ok = False
                break  # Series: one failure = system failure
        if all_components_ok:
            system_up += 1
    return system_up / trials * 100

The Weakest Link Problem

The last scenario ("one component at 95%") shows the brutal truth: your system's availability is capped by its least reliable component. It doesn't matter if 9 services are at five nines — if one dependency is at 95%, the whole system can't exceed 95%. In interviews, always ask: "what's the weakest link in this architecture?" That's where your reliability investment should go first.

Interview Questions & Answers

Actual System Design Interview

A Prompt You Could Actually Get

Design a payment-facing API for 99.99% monthly availability. The hard part is not saying "multi-AZ." It is turning that target into dependency math, failure isolation, error-budget decisions, and a recovery plan that still works during a regional event. [1] [2] [4]

How I'd Structure the Answer

I would start with the SLI: for example, successful authorization responses inside a latency bound. Then I would turn 99.99% into an error budget and make the obvious point that every hard dependency spends from that same budget. Once you say that out loud, one load balancer or one regional control plane stops looking like a small detail. I would also separate data-plane and control-plane availability, because many real outages break coordination long before they break serving capacity. If traffic is partitioned by merchant, account, or card range, I would name that key and explain how failover updates routing cleanly. [1] [4] [6]

Where the Interviewer Pushes

The next question is usually: what happens during a partition or a partial regional failure? That is where CAP stops being abstract. I would say which operations fail closed for correctness and which ones can limp along on a degraded path. I would also expect a follow-up on drills. If the design assumes promotion or regional evacuation, I want to know it has been exercised, not just drawn in a diagram. In partitioned systems, stale endpoint maps, cold caches, and routing disagreement can still burn the latency and error budget even when the service looks "up" from far away. [1] [2] [3] [6]

Q1: What does "five nines" availability mean and how much downtime does it allow?

Five nines means 99.999% uptime. Per year, that allows only 5.26 minutes of downtime. Per month, ~26 seconds. Achieving this requires: no single points of failure, multi-AZ deployment (preferably multi-region), automated sub-minute failover, 24/7 on-call with instant response, and extreme discipline in deployment practices. Few systems outside of core infrastructure (DNS, payment rails, emergency services) genuinely need five nines. Most SaaS products target three to four nines (99.9%–99.99%).

Q2: What's the difference between SLA, SLO, and SLI?

SLI = the metric you measure (e.g., percentage of successful responses). SLO = the target for that metric (e.g., "99.95% of requests succeed per month"). SLA = the business contract that bundles SLOs and specifies consequences for breach (e.g., "if we miss 99.95%, you get 10% service credit"). SLIs feed into SLOs; SLOs compose into SLAs. Engineers focus on SLOs — if you consistently meet your SLOs, your SLA takes care of itself.

Q3: A system has three components in series, each at 99.9%. What's the system availability?

For series (all must work): A = 0.999 × 0.999 × 0.999 = 0.997 = 99.7%. That's below three nines, allowing ~26 hours of downtime per year. If you need three nines overall, each component needs to be individually higher — e.g., 99.97% each gives 0.9997³ ≈ 99.91%. Alternatively, add redundancy: putting each component behind a failover pair converts the series into a mix of series and parallel, dramatically improving the math.

Q4: How does redundancy improve availability? Give a quantitative example.

Two servers in parallel at 99% each: A = 1 - (1 - 0.99)² = 1 - 0.0001 = 99.99%. Two nines became four nines by adding one replica. Three at 99% each: A = 1 - (0.01)³ = 99.9999% — six nines. The gain can be dramatic, but it depends on independence. If both servers are in the same rack, a rack failure takes both out. Real availability gains require spreading replicas across racks, AZs, or regions.

Q5: What is the CAP theorem and how does it relate to availability?

The CAP theorem (Brewer, 2000) states that during a network partition, a distributed system must choose between Consistency and Availability. CP systems (e.g., ZooKeeper, etcd) return errors or stall rather than serve stale data — they sacrifice availability for correctness. AP systems (e.g., Cassandra, DynamoDB) continue serving requests with potentially stale data — they sacrifice consistency for availability. The choice depends on your use case: banking needs CP; a social media feed is fine with AP. Modern systems blur this line with techniques like eventual consistency, quorum reads, and CRDTs.

Q6: How would you design a system for 99.99% availability?

Eliminate single points of failure: Multiple app servers behind LB, replicated database with auto-failover, redundant load balancers. Multi-AZ deployment: Spread across 2+ availability zones so one AZ failure doesn't take you down. Health checks + automated failover: LB health checks every 5-10s, DB failover via Patroni/RDS Multi-AZ, DNS failover for the LB itself. Routing convergence: if the system is partitioned, version the shard or ownership map so every caller agrees on the current owner after failover. Safe deployments: Canary releases, automatic rollback on error rate spike. Circuit breakers: Prevent cascading failures from downstream dependencies. Monitoring + alerting: SLI dashboards, error budget tracking, PagerDuty alerts. Test your failover: Run regular game days / chaos experiments to verify everything works.

Q7: What is an error budget and how does Google SRE use it?

An error budget is the amount of unreliability your SLO permits. If your SLO is 99.95% monthly availability, your error budget is 0.05% of the month = ~21.6 minutes of downtime. When the budget is consumed (too many incidents), the SRE team can halt feature deployments until reliability improves. When budget is healthy, teams can deploy aggressively and take risks. This creates a quantitative feedback loop: the more reliable your system, the faster you can ship. The less reliable, the more you must slow down and fix. It turns the "reliability vs velocity" debate from an opinion war into a math problem.

Key Takeaways Cheat Sheet

The 10 Things You Must Know

1. Availability = P(system works when you need it). Measured as a percentage. Each "nine" = 10× less allowed downtime.
2. Five nines (99.999%) = 5.26 min/year downtime. Three nines (99.9%) = 8.77 hours/year. Know the table.
3. SLI (metric) → SLO (target) → SLA (contract). Error budget = allowed unreliability within the SLO.
4. Redundancy is the primary availability mechanism. Active-passive for databases. Active-active for stateless servers.
5. Series availability multiplies (gets worse): 99.9% × 99.9% × 99.9% = 99.7%. Every dependency is a tax.
6. Parallel availability compounds when failures are independent: 1 - (1-0.99)² = 99.99%. Isolation matters as much as replica count.
7. CAP theorem: during network partitions, choose Consistency (CP) or Availability (AP). Both have valid use cases.
8. The #1 cause of outages is software changes (bad deploys), not hardware failure. Invest in safe deployment practices.
9. Untested redundancy is hope, not a guarantee. Chaos engineering (Netflix Simian Army) turns hope into evidence.
10. Not all systems need the same availability. A personal blog ≠ a payment system. Match nines to business impact.

Diagram Interpretation

User Request → Load Balancer: The entry point. The LB distributes traffic and detects failed backends via health checks. If the LB itself dies, everything goes down — it's a single point of failure unless you have a redundant LB pair.

Server C (crashed): One of three app servers is dead. The LB detected the failure (health check timeout) and removed it from the pool. Traffic automatically redistributes to Servers A and B. Users don't notice — this is redundancy working.

Where pressure builds: Servers A and B now handle 50% more traffic each (3 servers' worth of load on 2). If they were already at 70% utilization, they're now at 105% — overloaded. This is why you need headroom. Auto-scaling would spin up a replacement.

Primary DB → Replica: The database has active-passive replication. If the primary crashes, the replica promotes to primary (failover ~10-30s). During that window, writes fail. This is the bottleneck — database failover is the slowest, riskiest link.

Where the real failure happens: The annotated "SPOF risk" boxes show it — the load balancer and the primary DB are the weakest links. Adding a second LB (active-passive with VRRP) and ensuring the DB replica auto-promotes are the two highest-ROI reliability investments.

Key Intuition

Availability is determined by the weakest un-replicated component in the critical path. A system with 10 redundant app servers but a single-point-of-failure database is only as available as that one database. Find the single points of failure, add redundancy there first, and test that the failover actually works.

References & Further Reading

Each source below is summarized by the specific reliability mechanism, outage pattern, or control-loop detail it teaches.

[1] Beyer et al. — "Site Reliability Engineering" (Google SRE Book, 2016)

Betsy Beyer, Chris Jones, Jennifer Petoff, Niall Richard Murphy. O'Reilly Media, 2016. Free online.
https://sre.google/sre-book/table-of-contents/

• Formalizes the SLI → SLO → error budget control loop: measure a user-visible indicator, set a target over a rolling window, and treat the remaining allowed failures as the budget for releases and risky changes.

• Makes the key distinction that an SLA is a contractual promise with penalties, while an SLO is the internal engineering target that should sit stricter than the SLA.

• Chapter 3 models reliability as an optimization variable: pushing the target upward reduces available error budget and therefore reduces the rate of safe change.

• Chapter 4 details good SLI construction: aggregate only events users can observe, avoid vanity infrastructure metrics, and evaluate targets over explicit time windows.

[2] Brewer — "CAP Twelve Years Later: How the 'Rules' Have Changed" (2012)

Eric Brewer. IEEE Computer, Vol. 45, No. 2, February 2012, pp. 23-29.
https://sites.cs.ucsb.edu/~rich/class/cs293b-cloud/papers/brewer-cap.pdf

• Restates CAP precisely: when a network partition exists, a replicated service cannot simultaneously preserve full availability and linearizable consistency for all operations.

• Emphasizes that the tradeoff is only active during partitions; outside partition mode, systems can and should pursue both high availability and strong consistency.

• Reframes CAP as an engineering spectrum by pointing to designs that vary semantics per operation, such as stale reads, delayed writes, or partition-specific degradation modes.

• Connects the theorem to operational design: partition detection, replica placement, quorum rules, and recovery semantics matter more than the slogan “pick two.”

[3] Rosenthal et al. — "Chaos Engineering" (Netflix, 2017/2020)

Casey Rosenthal, Nora Jones. O'Reilly Media, 2020. Original Netflix Tech Blog articles from 2011+.
https://principlesofchaos.org/

• Defines chaos engineering around a steady-state hypothesis: specify a measurable normal-output signal, inject a disturbance, and compare the observed state against the baseline.

• The principles document requires experiments to run in production-like conditions because retry storms, control-plane coupling, and autoscaling interactions often do not emerge in staging.

• Netflix’s fault models map to real failure classes: instance termination, injected latency, regional evacuation, and dependency blackholes.

• The technical value is not “breaking things randomly”; it is validating circuit breakers, retry budgets, failover automation, and observability under controlled faults.

[4] Oppenheimer, Ganapathi & Patterson — "Why Do Internet Services Fail, and What Can Be Done About It?" (2003)

David Oppenheimer, Archana Ganapathi, David A. Patterson. USENIX USITS, 2003.
https://www.usenix.org/legacy/events/usits03/tech/full_papers/oppenheimer/oppenheimer.pdf

• Classifies failure causes empirically and finds a large fraction rooted in operator actions, configuration mistakes, and state-management failures rather than simple hardware faults.

• Separates stateless front-end recovery from stateful back-end recovery, showing that outages involving naming, storage, and configuration services dominate downtime because state repair is slower than process restart.

• Recommends reducing mean time to recovery with restart automation, isolating stateful dependencies, and improving tooling around configuration rollout and rollback.

• The paper’s core systems lesson is that availability is controlled by operational complexity and state coordination, not just by adding more front-end machines.

[5] Amazon S3 Documentation and Incident Summary

Amazon Web Services. Official S3 docs, SLA, and the 2017 us-east-1 event summary.
Amazon S3 SLA · S3 durability and availability docs · 2017 S3 event summary

• The durability docs are worth reading for the exact separation of concerns: 11 nines durability comes from redundant multi-AZ storage plus integrity checking and repair, while availability is about whether reads and writes succeed right now.

• The SLA is the contractual layer, not the design explanation: monthly uptime percentages define customer compensation thresholds, which is why SLA numbers should never be read as the same thing as internal SLO targets.

• The 2017 us-east-1 event summary is technically useful because it shows a subsystem dependency chain: an operator command removed more capacity than intended, which forced restart of indexing and placement subsystems before request traffic could recover.

• A lot of availability failures start in metadata and control-path recovery, not in the object replicas themselves.

[6] Meta Engineering — October 4, 2021 Outage

Meta Engineering. Official engineering write-ups on the October 4 outage.
Initial update · Detailed follow-up

• The failure starts with a BGP withdrawal event: a backbone capacity-management command unintentionally removed route advertisements for Meta’s data-center prefixes.

• That routing loss propagated into dependent systems, breaking DNS authoritative reachability, internal service discovery, and engineer access at the same time.

• The deeper lesson is control-plane coupling: if recovery tooling, naming, and traffic engineering all depend on the same network substrate, one routing failure can block its own remediation.

• The write-up is worth reading for the recovery constraints, not just the trigger, because it shows why out-of-band access and dependency isolation are hard availability requirements.
